  • Picture elements

Voxels (3D)

  • Volume elements
  • Represent tissue volumes
  • Fundamental unit of MRI

Scalars, Vectors, Matrices, and Tensors

Neuroimaging data progresses from scalars to higher-order tensors as spatial, temporal, and multi-channel structure is introduced.

A matrix-vector equation commonly encountered in reconstruction is:

\[ A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \]

Fourier Transforms and Frequency Representation

Fourier analysis decomposes signals into frequency components, enabling MRI reconstruction from k-space measurements.

\[ X(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)e^{-j2\pi ft}dt \]

\[ X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n]e^{-j2\pi kn/N} \]

Critical Perspective: Limitations and Modern Context

Classical Fourier-based reconstruction assumes global sinusoidal basis functions, which are poorly localized in space. Sharp boundaries and transient features require many coefficients, increasing scan time and sensitivity to noise.

Modern techniques such as compressed sensing and parallel imaging address these limitations but remain mathematically grounded in Fourier theory. Mastery of these foundations is therefore essential for understanding contemporary neuroimaging methods.
